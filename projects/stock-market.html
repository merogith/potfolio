The question

Every investor eventually asks whether the market is "expensive", and whether that should change what they expect. This project tackles it empirically: across more than 150 years of real data, does valuation predict long-run returns, and how much downside risk do you take on by holding equities?

The data

Real, public market data spanning a century and a half: Robert Shiller's price/earnings/CAPE series, plus CBOE (VIX) and LBMA (gold) data, pulled with a reproducible script. The main cleaning decision: recent months store 0.0 as a placeholder for fundamentals that aren't reported yet, so treating those as real zeros would distort every average. I converted them to missing. This is long-horizon data with all the usual problems: regime changes, gaps, and inflation adjustment all have to be handled before any model is trustworthy.

Approach & method

  • Built a forward 10-year real-return series and loaded the cleaned monthly table into SQLite, using a LAG window function to rank the best and worst calendar years.
  • Regressed forward returns on starting valuation (CAPE) using OLS in statsmodels, reporting p-values and R².
  • Quantified the long-run real growth rate, the maximum drawdown on record, and the stock–gold correlation as a diversification check.

What I found

Once inflation is removed, the index's price has grown only about 2.4% a year. Most of the headline growth comes from inflation and reinvested dividends, so return expectations should be set accordingly.

Starting valuation has tracked future returns: months with a higher CAPE were followed by lower real returns over the next decade. The relationship is statistically clear, though it explains only a modest share of the variation (R² = 0.11). Today's CAPE of 30.8 sits in the top 5% of all readings since 1881.

Large losses are a normal part of the record, not an exception. In real terms the market has fallen as much as 81% from a previous peak (the early 1930s). That's the risk you accept for the growth.

Gold's correlation with stocks is near zero, making it a genuine diversifier that has helped cushion those deep drawdowns.

Valuation is a long-horizon signal, not a timing tool: it says a great deal about the next decade and almost nothing about the next month.

Recommendation

For a long-term investor, the data supports three practical steps:

  • Set return expectations around the below-average real returns implied by today's high valuation, not the recent past.
  • Pair equities with low-correlation assets such as gold or bonds to soften the drawdowns the record shows are inevitable.
  • Automate contributions and rebalancing, because the main risk the data exposes is behavioural: selling during the long stretches when the market is underwater.

Limitations & next steps

The regression is a simple linear fit, so its estimate at today's record valuation should be read as "below average" rather than a precise number. Useful extensions: a total-return series that includes reinvested dividends for a truer long-run figure, an out-of-sample backtest with confidence bands around the valuation signal, and a check of whether the same relationship holds across sectors and international markets.
