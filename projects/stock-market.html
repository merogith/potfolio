US Stock-Market Valuation & Risk

Does today's valuation tell you anything about tomorrow's returns? 150+ years of real market data, put to the test.

The question

Every investor eventually asks whether the market is "expensive" — and whether that should change what they expect. This project tackles it empirically: across more than 150 years of real data, does valuation actually predict long-run returns, and how much downside risk are you signing up for by holding equities?

The data

Real, public market data spanning a century and a half — Robert Shiller's price/earnings/CAPE series, plus CBOE and LBMA (gold) data. I loaded and shaped it in SQLite using window functions, then ran the statistics in Python. This is genuinely messy, long-horizon data: regime changes, gaps, and inflation adjustment all have to be handled before any model is trustworthy.

Approach & method

  • Built a forward 10-year real-return series with SQL window functions.
  • Regressed forward returns on starting valuation (CAPE) using OLS in statsmodels, reporting p-values and R².
  • Quantified the long-run real growth rate, the maximum drawdown on record, and the stock–gold correlation as a diversification check.

What I found

  • Valuation has historically tracked returns: a higher starting CAPE was followed by lower real returns over the next ten years, and the relationship is overwhelmingly significant (p ≈ 1e-40).
  • Today's CAPE sits in the top 5% of all readings on record.
  • Long-run real growth has been about 2.4% per year.
  • The worst drawdown on record was −81% — the risk you accept for that growth.
  • Gold's correlation with stocks is near zero, making it a genuine diversifier.

Valuation is a long-horizon signal, not a timing tool: it says a great deal about the next decade and almost nothing about the next month.

Why it matters

Beyond the finance, this is a demonstration of the full analyst loop on hard data: wrangling century-scale series, using SQL window functions, applying proper statistical inference rather than eyeballing a chart — and, most importantly, turning the result into something a person can act on (set realistic return expectations, budget for drawdowns, diversify deliberately).

Tech & skills

  • Python
  • pandas
  • statsmodels (OLS)
  • SQLite window functions
  • matplotlib / seaborn
  • Shiller · CBOE · LBMA data
