The question

A pharma sales team has finite rep time, and two questions decide how to spend it. First: do calls actually move prescriptions, or are reps simply visiting doctors who would have prescribed anyway? Second: which doctors should the team prioritize?

This mirrors my real work at Viatris, where I maintained healthcare-professional records across 70+ countries — so the project is a clean, shareable version of a problem I've handled with live data.

The data

A synthetic but realistic CRM dataset for 600 physicians — call activity and prescription volumes — generated by seeded scripts that deliberately inject real-world messiness (duplicates, missing values, mixed text and date formats, impossible values), so the cleaning steps are real, not decorative. Real pharma CRM data is proprietary and privacy-sensitive, which is why a reproducible generator was the practical path.

Approach & method

  • Cleaned and joined the call and prescription records, documenting every decision in a table.
  • Tested the call → prescription relationship with correlation and OLS regression.
  • Segmented the 600 doctors into four behavioral groups with KMeans.
  • Built a ranked priority list using SQL RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY region …) window functions.

What I found

Calls and prescriptions are meaningfully related: r = 0.49 (R² = 0.24, p ≈ 1.5e-37) — each additional call per year is worth about 5.5 more scripts. The effort isn't wasted.

Prescribing is concentrated — the top 20% of doctors write roughly a third of all scripts — which is exactly why which doctors a rep visits matters as much as how many visits they make.

A KMeans segmentation splits the doctors into four groups and isolates a 99-doctor "High-potential growth" segment: doctors with headroom who respond to calls but aren't prescribing at capacity — the best place to invest extra effort.

Performance also varies sharply between reps — the top rep drove 6,007 scripts against 154 for the lowest — pointing to a coaching and territory-balancing opportunity.

The analysis turns a vague "visit more doctors" into a specific, ranked list of 99 to visit first.

Recommendation

  • Reallocate calls toward the High-potential growth segment — these doctors have room to grow and respond to visits, so each call should return more scripts than visiting an already-loyal doctor.
  • Keep enough contact with the Champions to retain them, without over-investing where there's no upside.
  • Coach the weaker reps using the top reps' call-frequency and scripts-per-call patterns, and rebalance territory load toward higher-potential regions.

Limitations & next steps

This is correlation, not proof of causation, and it runs on synthetic data. To firm it up I'd add a call-to-script time lag (does this month's calls lift next month's scripts?), move toward causal evidence with a matched-control or A/B call experiment, and bring in cost-per-call so the recommendation optimises ROI rather than raw script volume.
